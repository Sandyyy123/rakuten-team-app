2The business caseSandeep Grover35s · 1:00
Script
Why does this matter? Rakuten France is a marketplace with millions of sellers, and every product has to land in the right category, because that is what drives search, filtering and recommendations. Doing it by hand does not scale and seller labels are unreliable, so we predict the category from the product's own text and image. The titles are free-form and multilingual, and about a third of products have no description, which is what makes it hard.
Cues: Why it matters; why it is hard at scale.

5Class imbalanceSandeep Grover35s · 2:50
Script
The first thing the data tells us is that the classes are very imbalanced, roughly fourteen to one between the largest and smallest, and a chi-square goodness-of-fit test confirms that is real, not chance. That is why we report weighted-F1, the challenge's own metric, alongside macro-F1 for the rare classes, and why every split is stratified so both sides keep the same balance.
Cues: 14.2:1 imbalance; chi-square; why weighted-F1 + stratified split.
6Missing data (MNAR)Sandeep Grover30s · 3:20
Script
About 35 percent of products have no description, and the missing rate varies strongly by category, so a chi-square test shows the missingness is informative, not random. Rather than drop those rows, we add a simple description-missing flag, so the model can use the absence of a description as a signal in itself.
Cues: 35.5% missing; MNAR; add a missing flag, never drop rows.
7Text lengthSandeep Grover25s · 3:45
Script
Text length varies a lot across categories and is heavily skewed, so we use a non-parametric Kruskal-Wallis test rather than a normal ANOVA. It confirms length differs significantly by category, so we keep the title and description lengths as numeric features.
Cues: Skewed length; Kruskal-Wallis; keep length features.
8LanguagesSandeep Grover30s · 4:15
Script
The corpus is mostly French, about 81 percent, with a real English and German tail. We use Wilson confidence intervals, which stay valid for the rare languages, to confirm those are genuine sub-populations rather than noise. That is why we chose a multilingual model later, so one model covers all listings without language routing.
Cues: ~81% French; Wilson CIs; motivates a multilingual model.
9Text preprocessingSandeep Grover40s · 4:55
Script
Here is the cleaning pipeline. Per listing, we merge the title and description, strip HTML with BeautifulSoup, remove URLs, emails and numbers, tokenize, and remove French, English and German stopwords. All of that is per-row, so it is safe to run before the split. TF-IDF, which learns corpus-wide statistics, is fit only on the training set and then applied to the test set, so there is no leakage.
Cues: Clean per-row before split; TF-IDF fit on train only (no leakage).
10Word frequencySandeep Grover25s · 5:20
Script
This shows why cleaning matters. On the left, the most frequent raw words are all stopwords like de and la, which carry no category signal. On the right, after removal, the top terms are real content words like couleur and piscine, and those are exactly the features TF-IDF keeps.

11TF-IDF: the feature matrixSandeep Grover35s · 5:55
Script
TF-IDF turns each listing into a vector: term frequency times inverse document frequency, so rare, informative words score high and filler scores near zero. The result is a sparse matrix, rows are listings, columns are twenty thousand terms, about 99 percent zeros. Crucially, it is fit on the training set only, then applied to test, so the test set is unseen at fit time.
Cues: TF x IDF; sparse 20k-term matrix; fit on train, transform test.

13Classical baselinesJonathan Vints40s · 7:20
Script
Thanks Sandeep. I started with classical baselines on the TF-IDF features. The text is vectorised, compressed with truncated SVD, and the training classes are balanced with SMOTE. Then two models, K-Nearest-Neighbors and XGBoost, are tuned with a five-iteration Bayesian search and scored on weighted-F1. XGBoost reaches 0.76 and KNN 0.72, and that is the classical reference the transformer has to beat.

17XLM-RoBERTa (0.87)Jonathan Vints45s · 9:50
Script
The deep text model is XLM-RoBERTa, a multilingual transformer, fine-tuned on our corpus. It fits the eighty-one-percent-French text with its English and German tail, no language routing needed. We cap sequences at 256 tokens, which covers about 79 percent of listings, and we keep stopwords because the transformer was pretrained on running text. Light cleaning gives 0.87 weighted-F1, versus 0.82 with heavy cleaning, so less is more here.
Cues: XLM-R fine-tuned; MAX_LEN 256 (~79%); light 0.87 vs heavy 0.82.

So if we early-stop on loss we stop too soon; optimising for F1 instead gives us the better model. It is a good reminder that the metric you stop on matters.

21Image preprocessingThomas Maisch35s · 12:15
Script
Thanks Jonathan. Most Rakuten photos sit inside a large white border, so the first step is a corner-crop: we average the four corner pixels as a background reference, keep the pixels darker than eighty-five percent of it, crop to that box, resize to 224 by 224, and normalise. That focuses the model on the product rather than the background.
Cues: Corner-crop to the product; resize 224; ImageNet normalise.
22Backbone: EfficientNet-B0Thomas Maisch30s · 12:45
Script
For the backbone I chose EfficientNet-B0. It is about five times smaller than the ResNet50 baseline, 5.3 million parameters versus 25.6 million, and it is actually more accurate on ImageNet. So it is a strong, efficient starting point for transfer learning.
Cues: EfficientNet-B0: smaller than ResNet50 but stronger.
23Tuning the image modelThomas Maisch35s · 13:20
Script
I ran a roughly twenty-five-combination ablation over freezing, augmentation, class weights, samplers and the classifier head. Freezing hurt: the deep ImageNet layers need to adapt to product imagery. The final model is a full fine-tune, and it reaches weighted-F1 0.6515, well above the ResNet50 benchmark of 0.5534 and far above a CNN from scratch at 0.31.
Cues: ~25-combo ablation; full fine-tune wins; 0.6515 vs 0.5534.

25GradCAMThomas Maisch30s · 14:20
Script
GradCAM shows what the network actually looks at. For a book, the heat sits on the title band on the cover, so it is reading the cover text. Cues: GradCAM: attends to the title band / the object itself.
26Multimodal fusion (0.8984)Thomas Maisch45s · 15:05
Script
Now the fusion, which is our best model. We take the frozen EfficientNet-B0 image features and the frozen XLM-RoBERTa text features, concatenate them to 2048 dimensions, and train only a small head on top, about one million parameters, which is 0.4 percent of the network. It reaches weighted-F1 0.8984 as a five-seed mean. The image branch adds a real lift over text alone, concentrated exactly on the books, magazines and games where the picture disambiguates.
Cues: Frozen encoders -> concat 2048 -> small head (0.4%); 0.8984 (5-seed).
27Fusion training dynamicsThomas Maisch30s · 15:35
Script
Because only the small head trains, the fusion learns gradually and reaches its maximum at about epoch nine, while both encoders are already converged. So the head is really just learning how to weigh the two modalities against each other.
Cues: Only the head trains; peaks at epoch 9.
