2Business case

Sandeep
40 s1:00

Cues

  • Rakuten's marketplace holds millions of seller-uploaded listings, and the category is often wrong or missing.
  • Wrong categories break search, filtering and recommendations, so this is a revenue lever, not just catalog tidy-up.
  • Automating the classification removes manual tagging and keeps the catalog consistent as it grows.

Script (say this)

  • Why does this matter?
  • Rakuten France is a marketplace with millions of sellers, and every product has to land in the right category, because that is what drives search, filtering and recommendations.
  • Doing it by hand does not scale and seller labels are unreliable, so we predict the category from the product's own text and image.
  • The titles are free-form and multilingual, and about a third of products have no description, which is what makes it hard.

5Class imbalance

Sandeep
35 s2:55

Cues

  • We have 27 classes and they are far from balanced: the largest to smallest ratio is about 14.2 to 1.
  • A chi-square goodness-of-fit against a uniform distribution rejects uniformity strongly, so the imbalance is structural, not sampling noise.
  • Because of this we report weighted-F1 everywhere, since plain accuracy would flatter the big classes.

Script (say this)

  • The first thing the data tells us is that the classes are very imbalanced, roughly fourteen to one between the largest and smallest, and a chi-square goodness-of-fit test confirms that is real, not chance.
  • That is why we report weighted-F1, the challenge's own metric, alongside macro-F1 for the rare classes, and why every split is stratified so both sides keep the same balance.

6Missing data (MAR)

Sandeep
35 s3:30

Cues

  • About 35.5% of listings have no description at all, so this is a large gap, not an edge case.
  • The missingness is not random: it correlates with the observed category, which makes it MAR, missing at random.
  • Since the gap itself is informative, we keep it as signal rather than dropping those rows.

Script (say this)

  • About 35 percent of products have no description, and the missing rate varies strongly by category, so a chi-square test shows the missingness is informative, not random.
  • Rather than drop those rows, we add a simple description-missing flag, so the model can use the absence of a description as a signal in itself.

9Text preprocessing pipeline

Sandeep
35 s5:05

Cues

  • The cleaning chain: strip HTML, lowercase, remove punctuation and digits, handle accents, and remove stopwords.
  • We use multilingual stopword lists because of the language mix.
  • We keep two versions, a light clean and a heavy clean, and let the models tell us which one they prefer.

Script (say this)

  • Here is the cleaning pipeline.
  • Per listing, we merge the title and description, strip HTML with regular expressions, remove URLs, emails and numbers, tokenize, and remove French, English and German stopwords.
  • All of that is per-row, so it is safe to run before the split.
  • TF-IDF, which learns corpus-wide statistics, is fit only on the training set and then applied to the test set, so there is no leakage.

11TF-IDF matrix

Sandeep
35 s6:08

Cues

  • We turn the cleaned text into a large sparse TF-IDF matrix.
  • That matrix is the input for our classical baselines.
  • TF-IDF is fast and interpretable, a good floor to beat before bringing in a transformer.

Script (say this)

  • TF-IDF turns each listing into a vector: term frequency times inverse document frequency, so rare, informative words score high and filler scores near zero.
  • The result is a sparse matrix, rows are listings, columns are twenty thousand terms, about 99 percent zeros.
  • Crucially, it is fit on the training set only, then applied to test, so the test set is unseen at fit time.

12Classical baselines (KNN / XGBoost)

Jonathan
42 s6:50

Cues

  • Two baselines on the TF-IDF matrix: KNN reaches weighted-F1 0.72, XGBoost reaches 0.76.
  • XGBoost was tuned with a light 5-iteration BayesSearch, optimizing F1 directly, no MCC in the loop.
  • They are respectable, but classical models plateau around here, which is what pushed us to a transformer.

Script (say this)

  • Thanks Sandeep.
  • I started with classical baselines on the TF-IDF features.
  • The text is vectorised, compressed with truncated SVD, and the training classes are balanced with SMOTE.
  • Then two models, K-Nearest-Neighbors and XGBoost, are tuned with a five-iteration Bayesian search and scored on weighted-F1.
  • XGBoost reaches 0.76 and KNN 0.72, and that is the classical reference the transformer has to beat.

17Text training dynamics

Jonathan
32 s9:58

Cues

  • Training and validation curves stay close together, so there is no serious overfitting.

Script (say this)

  • This shows an important subtlety in training.
  • The F1 score keeps improving up to epoch 7, the last epoch of the run, while the validation loss bottoms out at epoch 4 and then rises.
  • So if we early-stop on loss we stop too soon; selecting the checkpoint on F1 instead gives us the better model.

18Class weights

Jonathan
32 s10:30

Cues

  • To handle the imbalance we weight the loss by class frequency during training.
  • This stops the big classes from dominating and helps recall on the rare ones.
  • It is a cleaner fix than resampling for a transformer at this scale.

Script (say this)

  • I also tested class weights.
  • On the left is per-class recall without weights, on the right with them.
  • The overall F1 barely moves, 0.87 versus 0.86, so it does not help globally, which is why the final model keeps weights off.
  • But the hardest, rarest classes clearly improve, so it is a real trade-off, and I report it as a negative for the headline but a positive for the difficult classes.

20Image preprocessing (crop)

Thomas
30 s11:38

Cues

  • Product photos often sit inside white borders and padding.
  • A corner-based crop trims that background and centers the actual product.
  • The result is cleaner, more consistent input for the CNN.

Script (say this)

  • Thanks Jonathan.
  • Most Rakuten photos sit inside a large white border, so the first step is a corner-crop: we average the four corner pixels as a background reference, keep the pixels darker than eighty-five percent of it, crop to that box, resize to 224 by 224, and normalise.
  • That focuses the model on the product rather than the background.

22Tuning the image model (0.6515)

36 s12:50

25Multimodal fusion (0.8984)

Thomas
46 s14:38

Cues

  • Our flagship result: late fusion of text and image features gives weighted-F1 0.8984, our best model.
  • We freeze both backbones and train only a small fusion head, about 0.4% of the parameters.
  • It converges by epoch 8, so it is cheap to train, and 0.8985 is a 5-seed mean, so it is stable rather than a lucky run.
  • It beats text alone at 0.87 and image alone at 0.65, the two views complement each other.

Script (say this)

  • Now the fusion, which is our best model.
  • We take the frozen EfficientNet-B0 image features and the frozen XLM-RoBERTa text features, concatenate them to 2048 dimensions, and train only a small head on top, about one million parameters, which is 0.4 percent of the network.
  • It reaches weighted-F1 0.8985 as a five-seed mean.
  • The image branch adds a real lift over text alone, concentrated exactly on the books, magazines and games where the picture disambiguates.
