1Title

Sandeep
20 s0:20
  • Introduce the three of us and the challenge: sort Rakuten France listings into 27 product-type codes.
  • Framing in one line: we combine the product title, the description and the image to predict the category.
  • Set expectations early - text does most of the work, the image adds a real lift, and fusion is our best model.

2Business case

Sandeep
40 s1:00
  • Rakuten's marketplace holds millions of seller-uploaded listings, and the category is often wrong or missing.
  • Wrong categories break search, filtering and recommendations, so this is a revenue lever, not just catalog tidy-up.
  • Automating the classification removes manual tagging and keeps the catalog consistent as it grows.

3A real listing

Sandeep
35 s1:35
  • Here is one actual listing: a short noisy title, a description that is sometimes long and full of HTML, and a product photo.
  • Point at the mess: mixed languages, marketing filler, and fields that are simply blank.
  • This is the raw material, and it explains why cleaning has to come before any modelling.

4The project at a glance (pipeline flowchart)

Sandeep
45 s2:20
  • Walk the flowchart left to right: raw listings, clean and deduplicate, then three tracks (text, image, fusion), then evaluation.
  • We model text and image separately first, then fuse the features late instead of training one giant end-to-end network.
  • Late fusion keeps each branch simple to train and to debug.
  • The whole pipeline runs end to end and is reproducible.

5Class imbalance

Sandeep
35 s2:55
  • We have 27 classes and they are far from balanced: the largest to smallest ratio is about 14.2 to 1.
  • A chi-square goodness-of-fit against a uniform distribution rejects uniformity strongly, so the imbalance is structural, not sampling noise.
  • Because of this we report weighted-F1 everywhere, since plain accuracy would flatter the big classes.

6Missing data (MNAR)

Sandeep
35 s3:30
  • About 35.5% of listings have no description at all, so this is a large gap, not an edge case.
  • The missingness is not random: it correlates with category, which makes it MNAR, missing not at random.
  • Since the gap itself is informative, we keep it as signal rather than dropping those rows.

7Text length

Sandeep
28 s3:58
  • Title and description lengths vary a lot from one category to another.
  • A Kruskal-Wallis test confirms the length distributions differ significantly across classes.
  • So the length of the text already carries information the models can use.

8Languages

Sandeep
32 s4:30
  • The catalog is mostly French, about 81%, with German, English and others mixed in.
  • We put Wilson confidence intervals on the language shares so the proportions are reported honestly, not as bare point estimates.
  • This multilingual reality is the reason we later pick a multilingual text backbone.

9Text preprocessing pipeline

Sandeep
35 s5:05
  • The cleaning chain: strip HTML, lowercase, remove punctuation and digits, handle accents, and remove stopwords.
  • We use multilingual stopword lists because of the language mix.
  • We keep two versions, a light clean and a heavy clean, and let the models tell us which one they prefer.

10Word frequency

Sandeep
28 s5:33
  • Before cleaning, the most frequent tokens are junk: HTML tags, stopwords and filler.
  • After removal, the frequent terms become real product words.
  • It is a quick visual check that the cleaning actually does its job.

11TF-IDF matrix

Sandeep
35 s6:08
  • We turn the cleaned text into a large sparse TF-IDF matrix.
  • That matrix is the input for our classical baselines.
  • TF-IDF is fast and interpretable, a good floor to beat before bringing in a transformer.

12EDA summary

Sandeep
32 s6:40
  • Recap: 27 imbalanced classes, heavy missingness that is MNAR, mostly French text, and plenty of noise.
  • These findings drove every modelling choice that follows.
  • One number to hold onto: we work on the deduplicated corpus, 83,502 listings after removing 1,414 duplicates from 84,916 raw.

13Classical baselines (KNN / XGBoost)

Jonathan
42 s7:22
  • Two baselines on the TF-IDF matrix: KNN reaches weighted-F1 0.72, XGBoost reaches 0.76.
  • XGBoost was tuned with a light 5-iteration BayesSearch, optimizing F1 directly, no MCC in the loop.
  • They are respectable, but classical models plateau around here, which is what pushed us to a transformer.

14Classical confusion matrices (XGBoost 0.76 / KNN 0.72)

Jonathan
38 s8:00
  • Side by side, both matrices show a decent diagonal, with XGBoost cleaner than KNN.
  • The errors cluster between genuinely similar product types rather than spreading randomly.
  • This tells us the ceiling here is the features, so a stronger language model is the next move.

15SHAP - a hard case

Jonathan
38 s8:38
  • SHAP breaks down one prediction the model gets wrong, token by token.
  • You can see which words pulled it toward the wrong class, usually ambiguous or shared terms.
  • It shows the failure is about overlapping vocabulary, not the model behaving randomly.

16SHAP - a correct case

Jonathan
32 s9:10
  • The same view on a correct prediction: the model leans on sensible, on-topic product keywords.
  • The tokens driving the decision are exactly the ones a human would use.
  • Together these two cases show the features are meaningful, not artifacts of the cleaning.

17XLM-RoBERTa (0.87)

Jonathan
48 s9:58
  • A multilingual transformer, fine-tuned, lifts weighted-F1 to 0.87, well above the classical baselines.
  • One finding to call out: light cleaning beats heavy cleaning here, 0.87 versus 0.82, the model prefers more raw context.
  • We set MAX_LEN to 256 tokens, which fits about 79% of listings without truncation, a good accuracy and speed trade-off.
  • This is our strongest single-modality model.

18Text training dynamics

Jonathan
32 s10:30
  • Training and validation curves stay close together, so there is no serious overfitting.
  • Early stopping keeps us at the best epoch.
  • The run is stable and reproducible.

19Class weights

Jonathan
32 s11:02
  • To handle the imbalance we weight the loss by class frequency during training.
  • This stops the big classes from dominating and helps recall on the rare ones.
  • It is a cleaner fix than resampling for a transformer at this scale.

20Text confusion matrix

Jonathan
38 s11:40
  • Strong diagonal: most of the 27 classes are predicted well.
  • The confusions that remain are between categories that really are similar.
  • This is the bar the image branch has to add something to, so over to Thomas for images and fusion.

21Image preprocessing (crop)

Thomas
30 s12:10
  • Product photos often sit inside white borders and padding.
  • A corner-based crop trims that background and centers the actual product.
  • The result is cleaner, more consistent input for the CNN.

22Backbone - EfficientNet-B0

Thomas
36 s12:46
  • We compared backbones and picked EfficientNet-B0 for its balance of accuracy and compute.
  • It comes pretrained on ImageNet and we fine-tune it on our catalog.
  • It is lighter than ResNet50 but performs better on our data, as the next slide shows.

23Tuning the image model (0.6515)

Thomas
36 s13:22
  • Our best image-only model reaches weighted-F1 0.6515.
  • For context, a ResNet50 gets 0.5534 and a CNN trained from scratch only about 0.31.
  • So transfer learning plus careful tuning is what makes the image side usable.

24Where the image model fails

Thomas
30 s13:52
  • On its own the image confuses categories that look alike, such as different kinds of books or accessories.
  • Photo quality and framing vary a lot from one seller to the next.
  • The image cannot separate these on looks alone, which is the case for fusing it with text.

25GradCAM

Thomas
32 s14:24
  • GradCAM heatmaps show the model attending to the product itself, not the background.
  • It is reassuring evidence that the model learned to look in the right place.
  • So when it fails, it is genuine visual ambiguity rather than the model being distracted.

26Multimodal fusion (0.8984)

Thomas
46 s15:10
  • Our flagship result: late fusion of text and image features gives weighted-F1 0.8984, our best model.
  • We freeze both backbones and train only a small fusion head, about 0.4% of the parameters.
  • It converges by epoch 9, so it is cheap to train, and 0.8984 is a 5-seed mean, so it is stable rather than a lucky run.
  • It beats text alone at 0.87 and image alone at 0.65, the two views complement each other.

27Fusion training dynamics

Thomas
32 s15:42
  • Convergence is fast and clean because most of the network stays frozen.
  • Variance across the five seeds is very small.
  • Efficient to train and reproducible, both properties we can stand behind.

28Fusion confusion matrix

Thomas
32 s16:14
  • The diagonal is even stronger than for text alone.
  • Several classes that text used to confuse get cleaned up.
  • This is the image pulling its weight exactly where text was ambiguous.

29Where the image helps

Thomas
32 s16:46
  • With frozen features across five seeds: image scores 0.6787, text 0.8761, and fused 0.8985.
  • Per class, adding the image gives an average gain of about +0.0224 in F1.
  • The lift concentrates in the visually distinctive categories, which is where an image should help.

30Text vs image vs fusion

Thomas
36 s17:22
  • Side by side: text is the workhorse, the image is weaker on its own, and fusion beats both.
  • Fusion is also the cheapest of the three to train, thanks to the frozen features.
  • Best accuracy and lowest training cost together is the headline of the image and fusion work.
