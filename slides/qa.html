Deep Q&A & Glossary

Rakuten multimodal classification (Liora team project)
How to use: the page is organised into three sections – exploration & text preprocessing, text modelling and image & multimodal fusion. Within each, questions are grouped by stage, and every answer explains the concept in full and, where there was a choice, says why this and not the alternatives. The orange ⚖ questions are the "why-this-not-that" deep dives. A glossary follows the first section. Statistics on the exploration data use the full training set (84,916 listings).
Exploration & text preprocessing
The data, the metric, class imbalance, missingness, text length, languages, cleaning, the split, and the TF-IDF feature hand-off.

1 · Business case & the data

In one line, what is the problem?
Predict a product's category (one of 27 prdtypecode values) from its text (title + description) and its image, so that Rakuten's search, filtering, recommendation and merchandising work.
Why is this a hard problem, not a trivial lookup?
Sellers write free-form, multilingual titles; 35% have no description at all; some products are mislabelled; the full catalogue has thousands of categories; and there are two very different data types (text and image) to combine. None of that is solvable by keyword rules at scale.
What does "multimodal" mean here?
Using more than one type of data for one prediction: text (title + description) and an image. My section handles the text side; the image and the fusion come later.
What is the target variable, exactly?
prdtypecode — a categorical label taking 27 distinct values (e.g. 2583 = pools & spas, 1180 = board games). It is a single-label multiclass problem: each listing belongs to exactly one category.
How big is the data, and how is it structured?
84,916 labelled training listings and 13,812 unlabelled test listings. Fields: designation (title, always present), description (optional, HTML-formatted), productid/imageid (image link), and prdtypecode (target). Text ~60 MB; images ~2.2 GB of 500×500 JPEGs.
Why does the messiness (missing text, many languages) matter to the business?
A mis-categorised product is invisible to buyers — lost sales and a worse experience. The messiness is precisely why manual labelling fails at scale and why an automatic, robust classifier is needed.

2 · The metric: accuracy, precision, recall, F1

What is accuracy, and why is it misleading here?
Accuracy = correct predictions / total predictions. It's intuitive but under a 14.2× imbalance it lies: a model that mostly predicts the big classes can post a high accuracy while completely failing the small categories. Accuracy hides per-class failure, so we never headline it.
What are precision and recall?
For a given class X: Precision = of everything the model labelled X, how much really was X — TP / (TP+FP), i.e. how few false alarms. Recall = of everything that truly is X, how much the model found — TP / (TP+FN), i.e. how few misses. They trade off: you can raise precision by only predicting X when very sure (but then miss many), or raise recall by predicting X liberally (but with false alarms).
What is the F1 score, and why the harmonic mean?
F1 = 2 · (precision · recall) / (precision + recall) — the harmonic mean of precision and recall. It is a single number that is only high when both are high. The harmonic mean (not the plain average) punishes imbalance: if precision = 1.0 but recall = 0.0, the plain average is 0.5 but F1 = 0, so you cannot game it by being good at only one side.
What is macro-F1? (the one people don't know)
Compute F1 separately for each of the 27 classes, then take the plain, unweighted average. Every class counts equally — the smallest class (764 items) counts as much as the biggest (10,209). This is the fair metric when rare classes matter: a model that ignores small categories gets a low macro-F1 even if its accuracy looks high.
What is weighted-F1?
Compute F1 per class, then average weighted by each class's size (its support). Big classes dominate the number, so it reflects the experience of a typical listing (most listings are in big classes). Its weakness: it can look good while rare classes quietly fail.
What is micro-F1, and why don't we lead with it?
Pool all classes' true/false positives and negatives together and compute one F1. In a single-label multiclass problem, micro-F1 equals accuracy, so it carries the same imbalance blind spot; not informative here.
So why does the Rakuten challenge use weighted-F1, and why do you also track macro-F1?
Weighted-F1 is the challenge's official scoring metric, so we report it to be comparable on the leaderboard. But because of the imbalance we also track macro-F1 as a health check on the rare classes: weighted-F1 alone could look strong while small categories fail. Reporting both is the honest choice.
What about ROC-AUC or log-loss?
ROC-AUC and log-loss are probability/threshold metrics, most natural for binary problems. For a 27-class single-label task with an official F1 metric, weighted-F1 (plus macro-F1) is the right headline; AUC / log-loss can be reported as secondary diagnostics in the modelling stage.

3 · Class imbalance

Why 83,502 products and not 84,916?
The Rakuten challenge provides 84,916 labelled listings. We ran a duplicate analysis and found 1,414 exact duplicates (same title + description, ~1.7%), which we removed, leaving 83,502 for all statistics, the split and the feature matrix. A further 2,651 (3.1%) share only a title but differ in their description, so they are kept.
How imbalanced is it, and how did you prove it?
The biggest class (2583) has 9,814 listings, the smallest (1301) has 693 — a 14.2× gap. A chi-square goodness-of-fit test (observed 27 counts vs a uniform distribution) gives χ² = 35,282 (p < 0.001), so the imbalance is real, not sampling noise.
Why does imbalance change the metric AND the training?
Metric: it forces us off accuracy onto weighted/macro-F1 (above). Training: we use class weighting (class_weight="balanced") so errors on rare classes cost more, stopping the model from simply ignoring small categories; and a stratified split so every class appears in both train and test.

4 · Missing descriptions (MNAR)

What are MCAR, MAR and MNAR?
Three missing-data mechanisms. MCAR (Missing Completely At Random): missingness unrelated to anything — safe to ignore. MAR: explained by other observed variables. MNAR (Missing Not At Random): missingness depends on the value/class itself. Ours is MNAR — whether a description is missing depends on the category.
How did you show it is MNAR and not MCAR?
A chi-square test of independence between "description present/absent" and "category" gives χ² = 42,953, Cramer's V = 0.72 (p < 0.001). Chi-square says the association is real; Cramer's V (0–1 effect size) = 0.72 says it is strong. Missing rates range from very low to very high across categories, so the absence carries category information.
Why flag the missingness instead of imputing it?
You cannot meaningfully generate a real product description, and imputing would destroy the signal — the absence itself is predictive (MNAR). A binary desc_missing indicator lets the model use that fact directly, for free.
Why not just drop the rows with no description?
That throws away ~35% of the data, concentrated in exactly the hardest, description-sparse categories — it would make those classes even harder to learn. Never drop informative rows.

5 · Text length

Why Kruskal-Wallis and not a normal ANOVA?
ANOVA tests whether a numeric variable's mean differs across groups but assumes the data are roughly normal. Text length is heavily right-skewed (a long tail of very long descriptions), so that assumption fails and ANOVA would be unreliable. Kruskal-Wallis is the non-parametric alternative: it ranks all values and compares rank distributions, with no normality assumption. Result: length differs significantly across categories (p < 0.001), so we keep the title and description lengths as numeric features.

6 · Languages

Why Wilson confidence intervals and not the standard (Wald) one?
A confidence interval for a proportion. The textbook normal (Wald) interval is unreliable for small samples or proportions near 0 or 1 — it can even give impossible bounds below 0. That is exactly where the rare languages (German ~7%, Dutch, Italian ~1%) sit. The Wilson interval stays inside [0,1] and remains accurate there, confirming English and German are genuine sub-populations, not noise. This motivates a French/multilingual model (CamemBERT, XLM-R) in the modelling stage.

7 · Cleaning the text

Why BeautifulSoup and not a regex to strip HTML?
A regex like <[^>]+> has three failure modes here: it merges words (rouge<br>bleu → rougebleu), it does not decode HTML entities (&amp; stays as text), and it breaks on malformed/nested tags — all present in seller text. BeautifulSoup handles all three by design.
Why lowercase but KEEP French accents?
TF-IDF treats Television and television as different features, so lowercasing merges them and cuts vocabulary fragmentation. We deliberately keep French accents (café ≠ cafe): they are meaningful in French and distinguish real words, so folding them would merge distinct terms. (A BERT model skips this — sub-word tokenisers handle case and accents themselves.)
Why merge title + description instead of keeping two feature sets?
One merged field gives a single TF-IDF matrix with no extra hyperparameter. Two separate branches double the dimensionality for only a marginal gain. Merging keeps it simple and effective.

8 · Split, leakage & overfitting

Why do we split the data at all?
The whole point is to estimate how the model will do on data it has never seen. If you score a model on the same rows it trained on, a model that simply memorised them gets a near-perfect score that means nothing in production. Holding out a test set the model never touches during training gives an honest measure of generalisation and is the only way to detect overfitting.
Why an 80/20 split and not 60/20/20 with a validation set?
At this stage we only need a held-out test set to measure generalisation, so an 80/20 split maximises training data. A dedicated validation set (or cross-validation) for hyperparameter tuning belongs to the modelling stage, so we don't spend rows on it here.
Why a stratified split and not a random one?
Under a 14.2× imbalance, a plain random split could leave a rare class (764 items) almost entirely on one side. Stratifying on prdtypecode preserves each class's proportion in both train and test.
What is data leakage, and where is the risk here?
Leakage is when information from the test set (or the future) sneaks into training, giving an over-optimistic score that won't hold in production. Our risk: if TF-IDF learned its vocabulary and IDF weights from all the data, the test-set words would shape the features. We fit TF-IDF on the training split only, then transform the test split with those frozen weights.
What is overfitting vs underfitting, and how do you guard against it?
Overfitting: the model memorises the training data's noise, scoring high on train but poorly on unseen data. Underfitting: the model is too simple to capture the pattern and does poorly everywhere. Guards: a held-out test set, fitting all transforms on train only (no leakage), and — in the modelling stage — regularisation and cross-validation. In this stage, the fit-on-train + 20% held-out test is the safeguard.

9 · TF-IDF, bigrams & the feature matrix

What is TF-IDF (TF and IDF)?
TF (term frequency) = how often a word appears in one listing. IDF (inverse document frequency) = log(N / documents containing the word), how rare the word is across the corpus. Score = TF × IDF. Words in almost every listing (de) get IDF ~0 and are ignored; rare, telling words (nintendo) get high IDF and dominate.
Why TF-IDF and not plain word counts (bag-of-words) or one-hot?
Raw counts / one-hot treat every word equally, so ubiquitous stopwords dominate the vector. TF-IDF's IDF term down-weights common words and up-weights rare, discriminative ones — a much better signal for the same sparse cost, with no training required.
Why TF-IDF and not word2vec / BERT embeddings at this stage?
This stage's job is a strong, fast, transparent baseline representation. TF-IDF is deterministic, needs no training, and is a proven strong baseline on short product text. Dense/contextual embeddings (word2vec, CamemBERT) are the modelling-stage upgrade — that is Jonathan Vints's part.
What is fine-tuning (the deep-learning path)?
Fine-tuning takes a model that was already pre-trained on huge general text — e.g. CamemBERT, pre-trained on billions of French words — and continues training it on our specific data and 27 categories for a few epochs. The model arrives already "knowing" French, so it adapts with far less data than training from scratch. That is the deep-learning route in the modelling stage, contrasted with our TF-IDF baseline, which needs no training at all. Fine-tuning usually wins on accuracy but costs compute and transparency.
What is a bigram, and why 1–2 grams and not trigrams?
A bigram is two adjacent words, e.g. jeux video. Some category signal lives only in the pair — "jeux video" (video games) is a marker neither word gives alone. Trigrams (1–3 grams) explode the vocabulary for little gain on short titles and slow fitting, so 1–2 grams is the sweet spot.
Why min_df = 3 and why cap at 20,000 features?
min_df = 3: a term must appear in at least 3 documents; a word in only 1–2 is almost always a typo or one-off serial number that won't generalise. 20,000 cap: the full 1–2-gram vocabulary is ~280,000, mostly that noise; 20k keeps the useful French/English terms, and going higher buys ~1 point for far more memory.
What does the matrix look like, and what is a sparse matrix?
Rows = listings, columns = 20,000 terms, cells = TF-IDF scores. Both train and test are transformed with the same train-fitted vocabulary.
What does "sparse" mean, and how does it affect things?
A listing contains only a handful of the 20,000 vocabulary terms, so ~99% of the cells are zero. A sparse matrix stores only the non-zero entries (in CSR format) instead of every cell. Effect: (1) Memory — ~15 MB instead of the many gigabytes a dense 68,000×20,000 matrix of floats would need. (2) Speed — the linear algebra skips the zeros, so training is far faster. (3) Compatibility — the classical models (logistic regression, linear SVM) operate directly on sparse matrices, so nothing has to be densified.
What exactly do you hand over?
A numeric feature set: ~67,900 training and ~17,000 test rows, comprising the 20,000 sparse TF-IDF features plus the desc_missing and length features, over the 27 target categories.

10 · Tougher follow-ups an examiner might ask

Your χ² is 35,282 — isn't that huge just because n is large? Any big dataset is "significant".
Correct, and that is the key subtlety: chi-square grows with sample size, so with 83,502 rows almost anything reaches p < 0.001. That is exactly why we do not stop at the p-value — we report Cramer's V (0.72), a sample-size-independent effect size. The p-value says the association is real; V says it is strong. The imbalance we back up with the actual 14.2× ratio, not just the χ².
How reliable is the language detection on such short titles?
Honestly, language detection on short titles is noisy — a two-word title can be misread. So we treat the percentages as approximate and bound them with Wilson 95% CIs rather than quoting them as exact. The robust conclusion is directional: French dominates, with a real English/German tail — which is all we need to justify a multilingual-aware model. (The exact figures differ slightly depending on whether you measure titles only or the full merged text.)
TF-IDF is bag-of-words — doesn't it throw away word order and meaning?
Yes: pure TF-IDF ignores order, so "dog food" and "food dog" look identical. Bigrams recover some local order (adjacent pairs), which is why we use 1–2 grams. Full word order and semantics need sequence models (RNNs, transformers) — that is the deep-learning upgrade in the modelling stage. TF-IDF is deliberately the transparent, strong baseline, not the last word.
What happens to test-set words that never appeared in training (out-of-vocabulary)?
They receive weight 0 — they are simply not in the fixed, train-fitted vocabulary, so they contribute nothing. This is the correct, leak-free behaviour: an unseen word has no trained weight. It is also why fitting the vectorizer on train only matters.
What text cleaning did you apply (tokenisation, stopwords, stemming)?
The full chain, per listing: strip HTML with BeautifulSoup, lowercase, keep French accents (they are meaningful), tokenise with NLTK's TweetTokenizer, remove French + English + German stopwords, drop one-character tokens, and apply French Snowball stemming for the classical TF-IDF branch. An unstemmed copy is kept for the deep-learning branch, because transformers need raw words and stemming would damage brand/model names there. Stopword removal + stemming shrink the vocabulary and sharpen the linear model; min_df=3 and the 20,000-feature cap prune the rare tail.
What is NLTK?
NLTK (Natural Language Toolkit) is the standard Python library for classical text processing. We use it for three things: the TweetTokenizer (splitting text into word tokens robustly, even with punctuation/emojis), the multilingual stopword lists (French + English + German), and the Snowball stemmer.
What is stemming, and why Snowball (not lemmatisation)?
Stemming chops a word to its root so inflections collapse into one feature: jouer, joue, jouait → jou. Without it, TF-IDF would treat each form as a separate column, fragmenting the vocabulary. Snowball (Porter2) is a fast, rule-based stemmer with a French variant, which we use for the classical branch. Lemmatisation is the smarter cousin (maps to the real dictionary word using grammar), but it is heavier and fragile across three languages, so stemming is the pragmatic choice here. The transformer branch skips stemming entirely, since sub-word models handle morphology themselves.
What exactly is a sparse matrix, and why ~99% zeros?
Each listing has only a few dozen distinct words, but the vocabulary has 20,000 columns — so almost every cell is 0. A sparse matrix stores only the non-zero entries (value + position) instead of the full grid, cutting memory from ~1.3 billion numbers to a few million. scikit-learn returns TF-IDF as a scipy sparse (CSR) matrix, and the linear models consume it directly.
Are the TF-IDF vectors normalised? What does the cell value actually hold?
Yes — scikit-learn L2-normalises each row to unit length by default, so a long description and a short title are comparable and no document dominates by sheer size. So the raw TF×IDF weight for a term (e.g. IDF 5.6 for nintendo) is the importance, but the stored cell is that value after L2 normalisation — a number between 0 and 1. This normalisation is also what makes cosine-style comparisons between documents well-behaved downstream.
20,000 features is huge — isn't that the curse of dimensionality?
It is high-dimensional, but crucially sparse (each document touches only a few dozen columns), and linear models on sparse text (LinearSVC, logistic regression) handle tens of thousands of features very well — text is the classic case where high dimensionality is a feature, not a bug. We still control it deliberately: min_df=3 drops noise terms and the 20,000-feature cap keeps only the most useful vocabulary. It is also why we avoid distance-based methods (k-NN) and naive SMOTE here, which degrade in high-dimensional sparse space.
What is sublinear TF, and did you use it?
Yes. Sublinear TF replaces the raw count with 1 + log(tf), so a word repeated 30 times in a long description does not count 30× a word appearing once in a short title. It stops long descriptions from drowning out short, informative titles.
Why not oversample / SMOTE the rare classes to fix the imbalance?
SMOTE interpolates between feature vectors, which is ill-defined on a 20,000-dimensional sparse text representation and can create meaningless "average" documents. Naive text oversampling just duplicates rows and encourages overfitting. Class weighting achieves the same goal — making rare classes matter — without inventing fake data.
What is your baseline to compare against?
A majority-class classifier (always predict the biggest category) is the trivial floor: it gets modest accuracy but a very low macro-F1, which shows the task is genuinely non-trivial and that any real model must earn its per-class score.
A single 80/20 split gives one number — isn't that fragile?
A single split gives one point estimate with no variance. That is fine for this preprocessing hand-off, but the modelling stage uses cross-validation (rotating folds) to get a more robust estimate and a sense of variance before trusting a score.
What are the main limitations of your preprocessing?
TF-IDF ignores word order and semantics; it cannot see that "console" and "PlayStation" are related; language detection on short text is noisy; and the representation is fixed, not learned. These are exactly the gaps the contextual models (CamemBERT/XLM-R, fine-tuned) close in the modelling stage — my job is to give them a clean, honest, leak-free starting point.

Glossary / vocabulary

Corpus
The full collection of text documents (all product listings).
Token / term
A single unit of text after splitting, usually a word.
n-gram
A run of n consecutive tokens. Unigram = 1 word, bigram = 2.
Stopword
A very common, low-information word (de, la, et, the).
Vocabulary
The set of distinct terms the model knows; capped at 20,000.
Hapax legomenon
A term appearing exactly once (typos, serial numbers).
TF (term frequency)
How often a term appears in one document.
IDF
log(N / documents with the term); high for rare, telling words.
TF-IDF
TF × IDF; converts text into a weighted numeric vector.
Bag-of-words / one-hot
Text as raw word counts / presence, without IDF weighting.
Sparse matrix
Stored as only its non-zero entries (~99% zeros here).
min_df
Minimum documents a term must appear in to be kept (3).
Accuracy
Correct predictions / total; misleading under imbalance.
Precision
Of items labelled X, how many were truly X (few false alarms).
Recall
Of true X items, how many were found (few misses).
F1 score
Harmonic mean of precision and recall.
Macro-F1
F1 averaged equally over all classes (rare = big).
Weighted-F1
F1 averaged by class size; the challenge's metric.
Micro-F1
One F1 over pooled counts; equals accuracy (single-label).
Class weighting
Making rare-class errors cost more to counter imbalance.
p-value
Probability of a result this extreme if there were no effect.
Chi-square (χ²) test
Compares observed vs expected counts.
Cramer's V
Effect size 0–1 for categorical association.
MCAR / MAR / MNAR
Missing completely-at-random / at-random / not-at-random (ours: MNAR).
Kruskal-Wallis
Non-parametric test across groups (skewed data).
ANOVA
Parametric test of means across groups; assumes normality.
Wilson 95% CI
Proportion CI valid for small/extreme proportions.
Stratified split
Train/test split preserving class proportions in both.
Data leakage
Test/future information influencing training.
Overfitting / Underfitting
Learns noise (train-only) / too simple to learn.
Regularisation
Penalising complexity to reduce overfitting.
Cross-validation
Rotating train/validation folds to tune/evaluate robustly.
prdtypecode
The target: the 27-value product-type code.
Text modelling
From TF-IDF classical baselines (KNN, XGBoost) to a fine-tuned multilingual transformer (XLM-RoBERTa). All scores are weighted-F1.

1 · Classical baselines: TF-IDF, SVD, SMOTE

Why build classical baselines at all (TF-IDF, then truncated SVD, then SMOTE), when a transformer was available?
A classical baseline is a fast, transparent floor that shows how hard the task really is before spending GPU time. The pipeline is TF-IDF on the 20,000-term matrix, then truncated SVD to compress those 20,000 sparse dimensions into a few hundred dense ones (latent semantic analysis: it denoises and makes the matrix usable by distance-based and tree-based models), then SMOTE to rebalance the 27 classes. KNN and XGBoost on that representation set an honest bar the transformer has to beat, and they isolate the value of the deep model: if linear algebra already scored 0.87, XLM-RoBERTa would not be worth its cost.
Why SMOTE, and why only on the training split?
The catalogue is imbalanced (the same 14.2× gap Sandeep measured), so minority classes are under-represented. SMOTE synthesises new minority examples by interpolating between near neighbours in the dense SVD space, pushing the classifier to attend to rare classes. It runs on the training split only: synthesising into the validation or test set would let the model see interpolated copies of the very rows it is scored on, which is leakage and inflates the score. Fit the resampler on train, score on untouched val/test.
Which classical models did you use, how were they tuned, and how did they score?
Two: K-Nearest-Neighbours and XGBoost, both on the SVD-reduced, SMOTE-balanced features. Each was tuned with a 5-iteration BayesSearchCV (Bayesian hyperparameter search, which spends its few trials where the score looks most promising rather than a blanket grid), scored on weighted-F1 to match the challenge metric. KNN 0.72, XGBoost 0.76. XGBoost wins because gradient-boosted trees handle the mix of dense SVD components better than a pure distance metric.

2 · Deep model: XLM-RoBERTa

Why XLM-RoBERTa and not a French-specific model like CamemBERT or FlauBERT, when the corpus is about 81% French?
The corpus is ~81% French, but the remaining ~19% is English and German, and a French-only model (CamemBERT, FlauBERT) handles the majority while degrading on that tail. XLM-RoBERTa is multilingual by design (pretrained on 100 languages), so one model covers French, English and German with no per-language routing and no fragile language-detection step on short titles. For a mixed-language catalogue the multilingual model is the safer single choice, and it still reaches 0.87.
Why MAX_LEN = 256 and not 512?
Product text is short (a title plus an often-missing description). About 79% of texts fit under 256 SentencePiece tokens, so 256 keeps almost all the real signal. Transformer compute scales with sequence length, so 512 mostly adds padding, roughly doubling cost for a fraction of a point. 256 is where you stop paying for empty tokens.
What is the XLM-RoBERTa architecture and training recipe, and what did it score?
Backbone xlm-roberta-base. The embeddings are frozen and all 12 encoder layers are trainable (FREEZE_LAYERS = 0), so the language knowledge adapts to product text while the token table stays fixed. The head is dropout 0.3 then a single linear layer 768→27 on the CLS token. Training: AdamW lr 2e-5, batch 16, gradient clipping, ReduceLROnPlateau (halve the rate when validation stalls) and early stopping that restores the best checkpoint, not the last. Result: weighted-F1 0.87, well above the text benchmark (0.8113).
Why does light cleaning beat heavy cleaning for the transformer, when heavy cleaning helps TF-IDF?
They read text in opposite ways. TF-IDF only counts tokens, so stopwords are noise and stemming merges housse and Housse into one useful signal, which is why the classical branch strips and stems (Sandeep's preprocessing). XLM-RoBERTa was pretrained on running sentences and uses word order and function words for context, so removing stopwords or lowercasing throws away information it was built to exploit. Measured on the same split: light cleaning (strip HTML, URLs and e-mails only) gives 0.87, heavy cleaning only 0.82. Matching the cleaning to the model, not one universal recipe, is the lever.

3 · Trade-offs, ablations & hand-off

Classical versus transformer: what is the trade-off?
XGBoost at 0.76 is fast, cheap, runs on CPU, and is directly interpretable with SHAP, a strong and explainable reference. XLM-RoBERTa at 0.87 is +0.11 weighted-F1 higher but needs a GPU, is slower, and is largely opaque. For this project the +0.11 justified the transformer as the production text branch, with the classical models kept as the transparent baseline.
What did you try that did not move the overall score, and did anything help a specific slice?
Three things left the headline flat: class weights, feeding designation and description as a sentence pair, and a linear-decay learning-rate schedule. None raised overall weighted-F1, and that is reported honestly. The nuance worth stating: class weights did not help the aggregate but did improve the hardest classes, the same imbalance trade-off Thomas sees on the image side (a support-weighted metric can hide a rare-class gain). So class weighting stays a tool for macro-level fairness even when it does not move the weighted number.
What did SHAP show, and how does the text branch feed the fusion?
SHAP was run on XGBoost to attribute each prediction back to specific tokens, confirming that concrete brand and category words (not stopwords or artefacts) drive the classical model, a sanity check that the features are meaningful. For the deep model, the XLM-RoBERTa text representation is the text branch of the multimodal system: Thomas concatenates it with the EfficientNet-B0 image features in a late-fusion head (see the image and multimodal section below). This section produces the strong, multilingual text vector the fusion stage combines with vision.

1 · Image preprocessing

Why crop at all? CNNs are translation-invariant.
Invariant to position, yes, but not to scale. If the product occupies 20% of the frame, the network spends most of its receptive field on white background, and the effective resolution of the product after downscaling to 224×224 is far below what EfficientNet expects. Cropping recovers that resolution.
Why 85%? Isn't that arbitrary?
It is a heuristic, and I would call it that honestly. It was chosen so that mild JPEG noise and soft shadows stay above the threshold while actual product pixels fall below it. It was not tuned; we ran no sweep over threshold values. That would be a legitimate improvement.
What if the product is genuinely white on a white background?
Then no pixel falls below the threshold and the fallback triggers: plain resize to 224×224, no crop. Same fallback if the bounding box is degenerate. The product is never lost.
Why augment at load time rather than saving augmented images?
A saved augmented image is a fixed transformation, so every epoch would see the same "random" flip, which is not augmentation. Sampling the transform inside __getitem__ means the model sees a new variant every epoch. (This is a mistake we made and corrected.)

2 · Model choice

Why EfficientNet-B0 and not the benchmark's ResNet50?
Higher ImageNet top-1 (77.1% vs 74.9%) with roughly a fifth of the parameters (5.3M vs 25.6M). Fewer parameters means less overfitting on a mid-sized dataset, and it was trainable on an M1 MacBook Air with MPS. On our own image-only task it also scored higher: EfficientNet-B0 0.6515 against the ResNet50 benchmark 0.5534.
Why not a larger backbone (B3, B7) or a Vision Transformer?
Compute. Running 26 ablation configurations on a laptop puts a hard ceiling on model size. A larger backbone is the obvious next step and would likely add a point or two on the image-only score. Whether it moves the multimodal score is much less clear: our fusion ablation shows the image contributes only +0.02 there, so improving the image branch has limited headroom in the fused model.
Did you use the pretrained weights correctly?
Yes, and specifically I did not hardcode the normalisation constants. timm.data.resolve_data_config() returns the exact mean, std and interpolation the checkpoint was trained with. Hardcoding them is a common silent bug.

3 · Ablation study

26 combinations on a 10k sample. How do you know the ranking transfers to the full 77k?
Strictly, I don't, and that is a real limitation. The assumption is that the relative ordering of techniques is stable with respect to dataset size, even though the absolute scores are not (0.5482 on 10k rises to 0.6515 on the full set). A stronger design would re-run the top 3 on the full data to confirm the ordering. We ran only the winner, for compute reasons.
One run per configuration, one seed. Isn't that within noise?
This is the fairest criticism of the image ablation, and I would concede it directly. The top configurations sit within about 0.02 of one another, plausibly within run-to-run variance. What I can defend is the gap between the top cluster (0.49 to 0.55) and the from-scratch CNN (0.31), and the fact that seed and sample were held fixed across all 26 runs. Multiple seeds with error bars is the correct fix, and it is exactly what we did do for the fusion ablation, where the conclusion actually mattered.
Why did freezing hurt? Transfer learning usually freezes the early layers.
Because the domain gap is larger than it looks. ImageNet is natural photographs; this catalogue is studio-style product imagery. Early layers (edges, textures) transfer fine, but the deeper semantic layers are tuned for ImageNet's object categories, not for distinguishing near-identical product packaging. Full fine-tuning lets those adapt, and with about 58k training images there is enough data to do it without collapsing.
Why didn't class weighting or the sampler help? The data is heavily imbalanced.
Because of the metric. The challenge scores weighted F1, which weights each class by support. Rebalancing improves rare-class recall at the cost of frequent-class recall, and under a support-weighted metric that is a net loss. Under macro F1 the answer would very likely flip. The trap to avoid is ever saying "class imbalance didn't matter": it did, we optimised for the metric we are scored on.
How did you ensure the runs were independent?
train_experiment() calls timm.create_model(..., pretrained=True) at the top of every run, so every configuration starts from the same fresh ImageNet weights. We hit and fixed this bug early: reusing a model object across runs means run n+1 inherits the trained weights of run n, and the ablation becomes meaningless.

4 · Evaluation

Why weighted F1 and not accuracy?
It's the challenge metric. Substantively: with 27 imbalanced classes, accuracy is dominated by the large classes, and F1 balances precision and recall per class before weighting.
Why is the confusion matrix normalised?
Row-normalised per true class, so each cell is a recall percentage. Raw counts would produce dark cells for the big classes regardless of how well they are classified, and make the small classes invisible.
Train F1 0.78 vs val F1 0.65: isn't that overfitting?
Yes, and it is visible in the loss curve: val loss bottoms out at epoch 3 and rises from epoch 4 while train loss keeps falling. Early stopping halts at epoch 6 and we checkpoint on best F1, not last epoch. Worth noting: train F1 is measured under augmentation and val F1 without it, so part of the gap is an artefact of that, but not all of it.
Your numbers are validation numbers. Are they comparable to the leaderboard?
They're measured on a held-out 25% of the training data. Directly comparable to each other, but not strictly to a leaderboard score computed on the 13,812-product test set. We'd expect a small drop.

5 · GradCAM

What does GradCAM actually compute?
Take the activation maps of the last conv layer (conv_head) and the gradient of the predicted class score with respect to those maps. Average each gradient map spatially to get an importance weight per channel. Weight the activations by those, sum, apply ReLU, and you get a coarse heatmap of which spatial regions drove the class score. Implemented with a forward hook (activations) and a full backward hook (gradients).
Isn't GradCAM post-hoc rationalisation? It always produces some heatmap.
Fair. It shows where the model looked, not why it decided. Its value here was diagnostic and negative: it confirmed the model attends to product regions rather than watermarks or background, and, more usefully, it showed that on the confusion pairs the attention sits on the silhouette rather than any distinguishing feature. That is concrete and actionable.
Did GradCAM actually improve anything?
Not directly, but it told us where not to spend effort. Once you know the model is looking at the outline and the outlines are identical, more image training won't separate those classes. That is what motivated moving to multimodal.

6 · Multimodal model

Why late fusion by concatenation and not cross-attention?
Concatenation is the simplest scheme that works and let us reuse both fully-trained branches unchanged. Cross-attention (letting text attend to image regions and vice versa) is the natural next step, and it's the honest gap in this section. That said, our fusion ablation shows concatenation extracts only +0.0224 from the image branch. If the image signal available to a linear recombination is that thin, I'd be surprised if attention found much more. Worth testing, not worth assuming.
You froze both branches. Isn't that leaving performance on the table?
Some, yes. Three reasons we did it: (1) both branches were already fine-tuned to convergence on this exact task, so unfreezing risks catastrophic forgetting; (2) backpropagating through 283M parameters on an M1 laptop wasn't feasible; (3) empirically it wasn't necessary, training 0.4% of the parameters reached 0.8984. It's a compute-driven choice that turned out to be near-free.
Were the branches really frozen?
Not in the strictest sense, and I'd rather say so than be caught. requires_grad = False freezes parameters, not buffers, so the BatchNorm running statistics of the image branch continued to update during training, because the frozen submodules weren't explicitly kept in eval() mode. Evaluation ran consistently in eval(), so the reported 0.8984 is a valid held-out measurement, and the parameter count (0.4%) is unaffected since buffers aren't parameters. The fusion-gain ablation doesn't have this issue: it extracts features under eval() and no_grad().
Why lr = 2e-5? That's very low.
Because the inputs to the trainable head are already-converged, high-quality features. A standard 1e-3 makes the head take huge steps in a space where the signal is already clean, and it overshoots. 2e-5 plus ReduceLROnPlateau keeps it stable.
Your multimodal model converges by epoch 9. Is it learning anything?
It's learning to combine, and there isn't much to learn, which is the point. Both extractors are frozen, so the representation is fixed; the only free parameters are a 1M-parameter head mapping 2048 features to 27 classes. Close to a well-conditioned problem. The head improves for a few epochs and plateaus by epoch 9, at weighted-F1 0.8984 (5-seed mean).

7 · Fusion gain ablation

How do you know the image branch contributes anything at all? Maybe the text does all the work.
We measured it, rather than assuming. We cached the features from both frozen backbones, then trained three fusion heads from scratch (identical architecture, split, recipe), zeroing one modality at a time, over 5 seeds. Result: text alone 0.8761, full multimodal 0.8985. The image contributes +0.0224, positive across all five seeds, with an SD (0.0006) far below the mean. Real, but modest. Text dominates this task.
But your text model scores 0.87 and the fusion model 0.8984. That's about +0.03, not +0.01. Where's the discrepancy?
Good catch, and it's exactly why the ablation was necessary. Those two models differ in two ways, not one: the extra modality and a much bigger classification head (a single linear layer on the text model versus a two-layer MLP over 2048 fused features). Comparing them directly would credit the whole gap to multimodality, which would be wrong. The clean attribution is the ablation itself, which holds the head and recipe fixed and isolates the image contribution at +0.0224 over 5 seeds; the head and architecture differences account for the rest of the model-to-model gap.
Why did you retrain the head rather than just zeroing the image features at inference?
Because those measure different things. Zeroing at inference feeds the trained head input it has never seen (out-of-distribution) and would understate the text-only performance unfairly. Retraining from scratch on zeroed features measures what the modality actually contributes, which is the question we wanted answered.
Your ablation's "both" is 0.8985 but your headline model is 0.8984. Which is it?
Both, and they're not in conflict. The ablation precomputes features, which means image augmentation is necessarily absent (a cached feature corresponds to one fixed augmentation draw), so the ablation's "both" isn't identical to the production model. What matters is that all three ablation configurations are perfectly comparable to each other, which is what an ablation requires. 0.8984 is the production number; 0.8985 is the ablation baseline, and the two agreeing to within 0.0001 is reassuring.
Only 5 seeds: is that enough?
For this effect, yes: the gain is positive in all 5 runs, with an SD of 0.0006 against a mean of 0.0224. The seeds vary head initialisation and shuffling, not the train/val split, which is fixed at random_state=42. So I can say the gain isn't an initialisation artefact. I can't claim it's stable across arbitrary splits; that would need repeated splits.

8 · Per-class contribution

+0.02 is small. Was multimodality worth it at all?
Two answers. First, the mean hides the structure: on some classes the image adds +0.09, which is not small. Second, +0.02 weighted F1 on 13,812 test products is real, and it's positive across every seed. But I'd resist overselling it. The honest headline is "text dominates, and the image contributes a small, targeted gain."
Where does the image actually help?
A small number of codes carry essentially the whole gain. Three dominate: 2705 (+0.094), 10 (+0.080), 40 (+0.067). Then 2403 (+0.031), 1180 (+0.026), 60 (+0.024), 1140 (+0.018), 1160 (+0.018), 2462 (+0.016), 2280 (+0.008), 2522 (+0.004). Everything else is either noise or negative. The challenge provider does not publish category names, so we deliberately don't speculate about what these codes mean; the result rests on measured F1 differences alone.
Some classes got worse with the image branch. How is that possible?
Yes: 2060 and 1280 at about -0.011, and 1920, 1560, 50 slightly negative, all robustly so. The fusion head learns a single global feature weighting, not a per-class one. It cannot decide to ignore the image features for one code and use them for another. Where the text branch already separates a code cleanly, the 1280 image dimensions act mainly as noise, and the optimiser, minimising global cross-entropy, accepts a small loss on those codes in exchange for the large gain on 2705, 10 and 40. It's a trade-off the architecture forces, not a bug.
Didn't you predict the image would rescue both weak clusters?
We did, and we were wrong, which is worth saying plainly. Our image-model error analysis identified two clusters of mutually confused codes: Cluster A (1140, 1180, 1280, 1281, 1300) and Cluster B (10, 2280, 2403, 2705). We expected the text to rescue both. The per-class ablation shows only Cluster B was right. In Cluster A, 1280 is robustly negative (-0.011) and 1281 is indistinguishable from noise, 2220 likewise. The corrected rule is: the image doesn't help wherever the image model was weak, it helps where the text branch is weak. Those are different sets of codes, and we'd only have found that out by measuring.

9 · Bigger picture

The best leaderboard result is about 0.9318. You're at 0.8984.
About 3 points, and I'd attribute it to: (1) no cross-modal attention; (2) a small backbone (B0), no ensembling, no test-time augmentation; (3) our text branch is frozen rather than jointly fine-tuned with the fusion head. Given that the image contributes only about +0.02, I'd expect the largest remaining headroom to be on the text side, not the image side, which is itself a conclusion the fusion ablation gave us.
What's your biggest weakness?
Two, and they're different in kind. Methodologically: the image ablation is single-seed, so the ranking among its top configurations isn't defensible, only the gap to the from-scratch baseline is. Architecturally: our fusion is concatenation, which cannot learn word-to-region interactions. The fusion-gain ablation is the part I'd defend hardest, because we did do it properly.
What would you do with another month?
In order: (1) multiple seeds with confidence intervals on the image ablation; (2) unfreeze the text branch and fine-tune it jointly with the fusion head, since the ablation says that's where the headroom is; (3) cross-modal attention; (4) a larger image backbone, which the ablation suggests is the least promising of the four.
Is the residual error fixable?
Partly, no. The challenge description itself calls the labels intrinsically noisy. Several of the codes we confuse most are ones where even a human annotator would struggle to draw a line from the image and title alone; the boundary is a cataloguing decision by the merchant, not a property of the product. I wouldn't put a number on that fraction without measuring it.

Things to avoid saying

Avoid: "The class imbalance didn't matter."
Say instead: we optimised for weighted F1, and under that metric rebalancing was a net loss. Under macro F1 the answer would probably flip.
Avoid: "GradCAM proves the model is correct."
Say instead: GradCAM shows where the model looked. It confirmed it attends to products, and it showed the failure mode is silhouette-based.
Avoid: "The multimodal model works because both modalities are strong."
Say instead: text dominates. The image contributes +0.0224, concentrated on a handful of codes.
Avoid: naming or interpreting product categories.
Say instead: the challenge provider publishes only numeric prdtypecodes, no category names. Any label seen elsewhere is third-party guesswork and may be wrong, so don't build an argument on it.
Avoid: "The image helps on all the codes the image model struggled with."
Say instead: this is the claim we disproved. It helps where the text branch is weak.
Avoid: overclaiming the image ablation.
Say instead: name the single-seed limitation before the jury does, and contrast it with the fusion ablation, which we did properly.
